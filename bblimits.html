Neutrinoless Double Beta Decay

Explained

Double beta decay A summary

Double beta decay, is the simultaneous occurrence of two beta decays whereby two neutrons convert to two protons, two electrons and two neutrinos via the weak force.

The Feynmann diagram on the right illustrates this process (time progresses from left to right).

Double beta decay was predicted in 1935 and has been observed in many isotopes.

Neutrinoless Double beta decay A brief theory of

Neutrinoless double beta decay, is a theorised mode of double beta decay whereby no neutrinos are emitted. This can only occur if the neutrino is a Majorana particle i.e. anti-neutrinos and neutrinos are the same particle.

The Feynmann diagram on the left differs from the one above as no neutrinos exist after the decay.

Neutrinoless double beta decay was predicted in 1939 and has not been observed.

Experimental signature

The summed energy of the two electrons emitted by double beta decay can take many values less than the total energy available for the decay. Whereas the summed energy of the two electrons emitted by neutrinoless double beta decay will be equal to the total energy available for the decay.

An simulated experimental signature for 150Nd decays is shown on the right for double beta decay in blue and neutrinoless double beta decay in red. Note the experiment is not considered perfect which leads to an extra spread in energies.

Neutrino mass Measurement

Observing neutrinoless double beta decay would not only state that neutrinos are Majorana particles but also allow a measurement of the neutrino's mass. This is because the rate of neutrinoless double beta decay, R, is related to the neutrino mass, m by,

R ∝ I G M2 m2

where I is the mass of the decaying isotope, G and M are constants that depend on which isotope is decaying.

Theory of G and M

G Is termed the phase space factor, and is related to the number of final states the electrons and nucleus can be in.

M Is termed the nuclear matrix element factor and is related to the probability of the decay occuring. Different theoretical models predict different values, leading to a range of possible neutrino masses for a single observed decay rate.

G Data from: J. Kotila, F. Iachello, Phase space factors for double-β decay, arXiv:1209.5722 [nucl-th]

Experimental aims Summarised

Neutrinoless double beta decay is a rare process, and therefore experiments that reduce backgrounds and/or increase the mass of the decay isotope will perform better. However due to cost and complexity constaints the existing and proposed experiments generally either maximise the mass of the decay isotope or minimise the backgrounds by improved energy resolution.

The experiments also choose different isotopes, based on cost, natural abundance, total decay energy and the predicted value of G M2.

Experimental results and predictions

As yet no experiment has observed neutrinoless double beta decay. The three most recent limits from active experiments are listed below,

Experimental predictions compared

As these experiments are background dominated it is possible to assume that the half-life limit will improve with the square root of the live-time. This allows a comparison of the current experiments (assuming they continue) and experiments that are due to take data in the coming years. Note that a comparison of the half-lifes is only safe for experiments using the same isotope.
